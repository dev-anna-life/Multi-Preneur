5 Essential Things to Check Before Buying Land in Nigeria

Buying land in Nigeria is one of the most significant investments you'll ever make. Unfortunately, it's also fraught with risks if you don't know what to look for. From fraudulent sellers to disputed titles, countless Nigerians have lost millions to preventable mistakes.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through the five most critical checks you must perform before purchasing any piece of land in Nigeria. These steps will help protect your investment and give you peace of mind.

1. Verify the Title Documents

The first and most crucial step is to verify the authenticity of the land's title documents. In Nigeria, the most recognized title documents include:

  • Certificate of Occupancy (C of O) - The gold standard of land ownership in Nigeria
  • Deed of Assignment - Shows the chain of ownership from one person to another
  • Governor's Consent - Required when buying land with C of O
  • Survey Plan - Shows the exact location and boundaries of the land

Always engage a qualified lawyer to conduct a title search at the Land Registry. This process involves verifying that the documents are genuine, checking for any encumbrances, and ensuring the seller actually owns the land.

Pro Tip:

Never rely on photocopies of documents. Always insist on seeing original documents and verify them at the appropriate government offices.

2. Conduct a Physical Inspection and Survey

Even with proper documentation, you must physically inspect the land. Many buyers have fallen victim to situations where:

  • The land is located in a flood-prone area
  • The property is inaccessible during certain seasons
  • There are existing structures or illegal occupants on the land
  • The boundaries don't match the survey plan

Hire a professional surveyor to:

  1. Confirm the land's location matches the coordinates on the survey plan
  2. Verify the land size and boundaries
  3. Check for any encroachments from neighboring properties
  4. Assess the topography and soil condition

3. Investigate the Community and Local Chiefs

In many parts of Nigeria, especially in rural and semi-urban areas, local communities and traditional rulers play significant roles in land matters. Before purchasing:

  • Meet with the community leaders and inform them of your intention to buy
  • Verify that there are no communal disputes over the land
  • Check if the land is designated for any community project
  • Ensure there are no cultural or religious restrictions on the property

Some unscrupulous individuals sell land without proper community consent, leading to future disputes that could result in demolition or loss of your investment.

4. Check Government Acquisition Plans

One of the worst nightmares for land buyers is purchasing property that's earmarked for government acquisition. To avoid this:

  • Visit the State Ministry of Lands to check if the area is designated for any government project
  • Inquire about the local government's master plan for the area
  • Check if the land falls within any restricted zones (military areas, airport clearance zones, etc.)
  • Verify that the land hasn't been marked for road expansion or public infrastructure

Important Note:

Even if you receive compensation for government acquisition, it's often significantly less than the property's market value and can take years to process.

5. Verify the Seller's Identity and Authority

Identity fraud is rampant in Nigeria's real estate sector. Many fraudsters impersonate landowners or forge documents to sell land they don't own. Protect yourself by:

  • Requesting valid government-issued identification from the seller
  • Verifying that the name on the ID matches the name on the title documents
  • Meeting the seller in person at their known address
  • Confirming the seller's authority if they claim to be representing someone else
  • Checking with neighbors or local residents about the seller's identity

If possible, trace the ownership history back several transactions. This helps ensure you're not buying from someone in a disputed ownership chain.

Additional Safety Measures

Beyond the five essential checks above, consider these additional precautions:

  • Use escrow services: Don't pay the full amount directly to the seller. Use a trusted escrow service or your lawyer's client account.
  • Beware of urgency: Fraudsters often pressure buyers to make quick decisions. Take your time to conduct thorough due diligence.
  • Get everything in writing: Ensure all agreements, promises, and conditions are documented in writing.
  • Consult multiple professionals: Don't rely on a single lawyer or surveyor. Get second opinions on major decisions.

Conclusion

Buying land in Nigeria doesn't have to be a risky endeavor. By following these five essential checks and maintaining a healthy skepticism throughout the process, you can significantly reduce your risk and make a sound investment.

Remember, the cost of proper due diligence is always less than the cost of losing your entire investment to fraud or legal disputes. When in doubt, walk away. There will always be other opportunities, but you can't recover money lost to a bad land deal.
